Octile Distance  ·  Connectivity_8

The exact shortest-path distance on an obstacle-free 8-connected grid — and why it is an admissible A* heuristic.

1 · Eight moves, two costs

From any cell you can step to 4 cardinal neighbours (N, E, S, W) or 4 diagonal ones.

  • A straight step moves 1 cell → cost 1.
  • A diagonal step moves 1 cell in both axes at once → cost √2 = √(1²+1²).
Why the big integers? Costs are stored scaled ×10 000: cardinal = 10000, diagonal = round(√2 × 10000) = 14142. Integers make every path-cost comparison exact — vital for detecting equal-cost (co-optimal) paths, which float √2 rounding would corrupt. Recover the true distance with cost ÷ unit (unit = 10000).

2 · The idea — go diagonal first, then straight

To reach a goal that is dcol columns and drow rows away (no obstacles), step diagonally until you line up on the shorter axis, then go straight the rest.

A diagonal step shrinks both gaps by 1; a straight step shrinks only one. So spend diagonals on the smaller gap, straights on what is left over:

dmin = min(drow, dcol)  →  diagonal steps   |   dmax − dmin  →  straight steps
A → B : 4 columns, 2 rows apart → dmin=2, dmax=4

3 · The formula

octile = √2·dmin + 1·(dmax − dmin)
scaled = 14142·dmin + 10000·(dmax − dmin)

For A → B above (dmin=2, dmax=4):

14142·2 + 10000·2 = 28284 + 20000 = 48284  →  ÷10000 = 4.8284 = 2√2 + 2 ✓

4 · How it compares

Octile sits between the two. Manhattan overcounts — it forbids diagonals. Chebyshev undercounts — it pretends a diagonal is free (cost 1). Octile is the true obstacle-free shortest path, so it never overestimates → an admissible and consistent A* heuristic, exact when the grid is open.
