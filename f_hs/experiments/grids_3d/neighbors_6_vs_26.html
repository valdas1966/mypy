3D Grid Neighbors: 6 vs 18 vs 26

How a voxel connects to its neighbours — and why the benchmark uses 26. Drag the cube to rotate.

1 · Start from 2D (what you already know)

In a 2D grid a cell touches its neighbours in two ways: sharing an edge (4 of them) or sharing only a corner (4 more). That is the familiar 4-connected vs 8-connected choice.

4-connected (orthogonal only)

8-connected (+ diagonals)

The whole idea in one line: 6-connected is the 3D version of 4-connected, and 26-connected is the 3D version of 8-connected. Going to 3D just adds a third kind of contact — touching through a single corner of the cube in space.

2 · The 3D cube — 3×3×3 around one voxel

The centre voxel (center) has 26 surrounding cells. They split into three families by how they touch the centre:

Count check: 6 faces + 12 edges + 8 corners = 26. Drop the corners and you get 18; drop the edges too and you get the 6 axis moves.

3 · Each family costs a different amount to step

Move cost = straight-line length between voxel centres (unit cells):

NeighbourCoords changeStep costIn the 6-conn world…
face1 axis 11 step
edge2 axes √2 ≈ 1.4142 steps (cost 2)
corner3 axes √3 ≈ 1.7323 steps (cost 3)
A single corner move (√3 ≈ 1.73) replaces three axis moves (cost 3). That is exactly why 26-connected paths are shorter and look natural instead of climbing a 3D staircase.

4 · Same trip, two path lengths

2D, corner-to-corner of a cell:

  • 4-connected: right + up = 2
  • 8-connected: one diagonal = √2 ≈ 1.41

3D, opposite corner of a cube:

  • 6-connected: x + y + z = 3
  • 26-connected: one body-diagonal = √3 ≈ 1.73

5 · The catch: no corner-cutting

A diagonal move is allowed only if the cells it squeezes past are free. The agent fills a whole voxel, so it cannot slip through the gap between two obstacles that meet at a corner.

✓ allowed

✗ blocked (would cut a corner)

Same rule in 3D: a face/edge/ corner diagonal needs its component axis-cells to be free.

6 · The matching heuristic

ConnectivityAdmissible heuristic2D ancestor
6|Δx|+|Δy|+|Δz| (Manhattan) 4-conn Manhattan
26 (√3−√2)·dmin + (√2−1)·dmid + dmax 8-conn octile

dmax≥dmid≥dmin are the sorted |Δx|,|Δy|,|Δz|. This is just the 2D octile distance with one extra term for the third dimension.

Why it matters for the benchmark: the released scenario files store each problem's optimal cost computed under 26-connected movement. Use 26 and you inherit those optimal costs for free; switch to 6 and you must recompute every optimal cost yourself.
