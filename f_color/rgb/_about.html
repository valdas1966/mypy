RGB

Three numbers. 16.7 million colors.

The Idea

Every color on your screen is a mix of Red, Green, and Blue light. Each channel is an integer from 0 (off) to 255 (full blast).

Key Combos

RGB is additive — you add light, not paint. Mixing all three at full gives white, not brown.

Facts & Trivia

  • 1861 James Clerk Maxwell produced the first color photograph using red, green, and blue filters — proving that three primaries are enough to reproduce any color the eye can see.
  • 1931 The CIE formally defined human color vision with three components, confirming what Maxwell demonstrated: we have three types of cone cells (L, M, S) roughly tuned to red, green, and blue.
  • 1953 RCA demonstrated the first practical RGB color TV. Before that, CBS tried a mechanical spinning-disk system — it was incompatible with existing black-and-white TVs and quietly died.
  • Why 256? 1 byte = 8 bits = 28 = 256 values. Three bytes per pixel was the sweet spot between visual quality and memory in early hardware. Below 256 steps per channel, you get visible banding.
  • Additive vs. Subtractive RGB is additive (light). Printers use CMYK (subtractive, ink). Mix all RGB light = white. Mix all CMYK ink = muddy black. That's why your printed photos never look quite like your screen.
  • Not all RGB is equal sRGB, Adobe RGB, Display P3 — they all use "RGB" but define different gamuts. A red of (255, 0, 0) looks different on each. Web browsers default to sRGB.
  • Fun The human eye can distinguish roughly 10 million colors — so 16.7M RGB values slightly overkill the eye, but not by much. Some values are perceptually identical.
  • Fun "Chucknorris" is a valid CSS color. Browsers parse unknown color strings by keeping only hex digits and padding — chucknorris becomes #c00000 (a dark red). Chuck Norris is red, obviously.
